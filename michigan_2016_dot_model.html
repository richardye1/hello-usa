Dot Map of 2016 Election in Michigan

Dot maps show political data without distorting by population. Regular county maps skew red, as more rural areas vote Republican, however this is misleading because these counties often have less population than more urbanized ones. Dot maps are an attempt to bridge this divide between geography and vote, and this map attempts to do so.

Wayne County (Detroit) lies in the bottom right, and is much larger due to its population. Notice the dark blue rectangle in this county, and this is Detroit, where Democrats ran up the vote through African American communities (which vote lobsidedly Democrat).

In contrast, you can notice the sea of lighter red that surrounds the blue cities. These are the rural areas, which have less "representation" vs a county map, because of population. However, most rural areas have a lighter shade of red; Rural Michigan tends to lean Republican but is not as republican as rurals in the U.S.

Trump's most likely path to victory lies in keeping the Detroit suburbs (Macomb, top right), while regaining many skeptical religious conservatives in Western Michigan (Ottawa, Kent). Trump's margins among rural areas must grow to offset losses in the educated suburbs and Ann Arbor/Lansing. It's a tall task but if there is someone best positioned to pull it off, it is our President.

Democrats have many options, and small events such as a turnout adjustment could easily flip the state that was decided by 11,000 votes (0.13%). Democrats should continue to campaign heavily here, something that was neglected in 2016. Michigan's junior Democratic senator is also up for reelection in a campaign expected to be fierce. Finally, turnout in Detroit could revert to normal from historically low participation rates in 2016. Even a small shift, albiet a few percentage points could add tens of thousands of votes for Democrats, crucial in a race decided by 11,000 votes. Every small shift matters and Democrats should not take anything for granted.
